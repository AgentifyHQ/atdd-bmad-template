Global temperatures have risen by 1.2 degrees Celsius since pre-industrial times, according to the latest report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). The report, published in March 2026, warns that without significant reductions in carbon emissions, the world is on track to exceed the 1.5-degree threshold by 2030.

Key findings include a 15% increase in extreme weather events over the past decade, accelerating ice sheet loss in Greenland and Antarctica, and rising sea levels threatening coastal cities. The report identifies the energy sector as the largest contributor to greenhouse gas emissions, accounting for 73% of the global total.

Scientists recommend immediate action including transitioning to renewable energy sources, implementing carbon capture technologies, and establishing binding international agreements on emission targets. The economic cost of inaction is estimated at $23 trillion by 2050, compared to $4 trillion for implementing the recommended mitigation strategies.

The report concludes that while the situation is urgent, the 1.5-degree target remains achievable if global emissions peak before 2027 and decline by 43% by 2030.
